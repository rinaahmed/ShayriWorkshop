Help & Manual

A guide to checking the behr of Urdu shayari — and just enough ʿarūz to understand what the app tells you.

Part 1 — The one idea behind the whole tool

Scanning a line of shayari has two completely different kinds of difficulty, and the app keeps them strictly apart.

The easy-but-tedious part: counting. Once you know how a line is pronounced, working out the weight of each syllable and matching it to a behr is pure arithmetic. There is exactly one right answer. The app does this with a fixed algorithm — the same answer every single time.

The genuinely hard part: pronunciation. Urdu script does not write short vowels. کل can be kal, kil, or kul; سہا can be sahaa or another reading entirely. You cannot tell the weight of a word from its spelling alone.

The app's strategy: lock down the pronunciation once, then let the machine count forever. The first time it meets a word, it asks Claude for a pronunciation, shows it to you, and you confirm or fix it. After that the word lives in your dictionary — no guessing, no internet, identical results every time.

Part 2 — Syllables and weight (the basics)

Short (S) — a consonant plus a short vowel, nothing closing it. One beat.

Long (L) — a syllable is heavy if it contains a long vowel (کا، کی، کو) or ends in a consonant (دل، کر). Two beats.

Over-long syllables. Some syllables have a long vowel and a closing consonant (روز، بات، ساتھ) or a short vowel with two closing consonants (درد، سبز). Heavier than a normal long.

  • Scanning by ear: feel one heavy beat — mark it L. روز is L.
  • In the fine arithmetic: the exact value is L + a leftover short (L·S, numbers 2+1). The app tracks this internally.

In Simple view (default) روز shows as one L tile. Switch to Detailed to see the overflow mora — useful only when a line is one beat off.

The end of the line is free (anceps). The very last syllable can be counted either way. The app applies this automatically.

Part 3 — How the app decides weight

The app looks at the pronunciation you've confirmed (ShayriRoman) and applies these rules:

The syllable is…Weight
short vowel, open (no coda) — ki, naS
short vowel + one coda — dil, karL
long vowel, open — jo, ko, beL
long vowel + coda — roz, bāt, sāthL (extra-heavy)
short vowel + two codas — dard, sabzL (extra-heavy)

Get the pronunciation right and the weight is automatic and always the same.

Part 4 — ShayriRoman: how pronunciations are written
SoundWrite

long āaa
long īii
long ūuu

ai / auai  au

a vs aa is the key distinction — short vs long. Most weight mistakes are a vowel length typed wrong.

Aspirates are ONE consonant: تھ = th, کھ = kh, گھ = gh, بھ = bh, پھ = ph. So اٹھا = uthaa, not uthhaa. The app warns you when it sees a doubled-h.

Nasalisation: ~ after the vowel — جاں = jaa~. Does not add weight.

Tashdid: double the consonant — محبت = muhabbat.

Part 5 — One spelling, several readings

Because short vowels aren't written, one Urdu spelling can map to several words with different pronunciations, weights, and meanings. سہا can be sahaa ("endured") or another reading of the same skeleton.

The app handles this honestly: a spelling can hold several readings in your dictionary, each with its own pronunciation and a short meaning note. When a line uses such a word, the app does not guess — it shows you the candidates and you tap the intended one. Your choice is remembered for that line.

Add a meaning note whenever a word could be read more than one way.

Part 6 — The two tabs and how you'll actually work

Behr Checker — type or paste a line, pick a target behr (optional), tap Check Behr. You get a syllable strip (rose = S, teal = L), Total Matras, Pattern, Closest Behr, and Feet Analysis (✓/✗ per foot). Tap ✎ on any word to edit it on the spot.

The Simple / Detailed toggle above the strip controls over-long display. Simple (default) shows روز as one L; Detailed reveals the overflow mora. Use Detailed only when hunting a missing beat.

Word Lookup — look up a single word's pronunciation and meaning.

Dictionary (⋮ menu) — your full word collection. Search, filter to Unconfirmed, Export/Import to back up your work, edit or delete any entry including confirmed ones.

Part 7 — Confirming new words

When a line contains new words, a "New words — please confirm" panel appears. Check the proposed pronunciation, fix any wrong vowel lengths, add an optional meaning note, then ✓.

  1. Long vs short vowels: kaa vs ka is the difference between L and S. Read the word aloud.
  2. Aspirate doubled-h: اٹھا = uthaa, not uthhaa. The app warns you when it sees doubled-h in the translit.
Part 8 — Pronunciation vs. weight override

Pronunciation (normal): give the ShayriRoman and the app derives weight. Use this for almost every word.

Override (rare): type the weight directly (e.g. 2 for L, 1 2 for S L). Reserve for small grammar words whose weight isn't predictable from sound: یہ، وہ، کہ، نہ، نے. A dictionary row shows [1 2] when an override is in force.

Only override when the pronunciation is already correct but the weight still comes out wrong. Otherwise, fix the pronunciation.

Part 9 — Reading the result

Total Matras — beat count (S = 1, L = 2).

Pattern — your line as S/L.

"N moras off" — 0 means the line sits cleanly in that behr; a small number means one syllable to fix; a large number likely means a different meter.

Feet Analysis — most useful for fixing. A ✗ foot shows expected-vs-actual. Fix the ✗ feet and re-check. The final syllable is always free.

Part 11 — Common questions & fixes
روز shows as L — is that right?
Yes. روز is one heavy long syllable. In Detailed view you'll see its overflow mora, but it counts as one L.
A line is one mora off and I can't see where
Switch to Detailed view to reveal overflow morae, then read Feet Analysis for the ✗ foot.
I confirmed a word wrong
Open Dictionary from the ⋮ menu, search the word, edit or delete it, and re-check the line. Confirmed entries are always editable.
Is the answer reliable run to run?
Yes — identical input gives identical output. The only thing that varies is a first-time proposal, which you lock down by confirming.
Part 12 — Keeping your work safe

Use Export in the Dictionary to save a backup file, and Import to restore it or move it to another device. The app and all confirmed vocabulary work offline — only the first sighting of a new word needs the internet.

This tool is meant to make you a better reader of meter, not to replace your ear. The more you teach it by confirming words thoughtfully, the more it confirms what you already hear.
